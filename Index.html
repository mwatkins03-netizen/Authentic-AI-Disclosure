Disclose your AI use

Authentic disclosure isn't a confession and it isn't a legal log. It's a short, honest account of how you worked with AI — written so a reader actually understands what you did. This builder helps you make a clean disclosure label and badge, while steering you away from the two traps: performative hedging and exhaustive cataloguing.

By Marc Watkins · modeled on Monash University
What authentic disclosure means

Honest, proportionate, human

Done well, disclosing AI does more than tick a box — it normalizes four things worth protecting in a classroom.

Transparency

Say what you used and how — clearly enough that a reader isn't left guessing.

Dialogue

Disclosure starts a conversation with your instructor — it isn't a verdict on your character.

Rapport

Trust is built when honesty is rewarded, not punished. Tell the truth and expect good faith back.

Shared Responsibility

You stay accountable for the final work. The tool helped; the thinking and the choices are yours.

Trap #1 · Avoid

Performative disclosure

Vague, defensive phrasing that signals honesty without actually telling the reader anything. It minimizes, hedges, and hides the real work behind a shrug.

"I just used AI to brainstorm…"
"I used Grammarly to touch it up, but the words are my own…"
"AI gave me some suggestions, but I did most of the writing myself…"

Do instead: name the tool, the specific task, and what you changed. Be concrete, not reassuring.

Trap #2 · Avoid

Exhaustive disclosure

The opposite failure: trying to log every keystroke and prompt until disclosure becomes unreadable. A 1,200-word essay does not need a 200-page appendix.

Yasha Hartberg's satirical proposal — that scholars "log every step of the process of composition" — shows how quickly this collapses under its own weight.

Do instead: summarize the shape of your AI use proportionately. Keep detailed notes ready if asked, but don't paste them in. See Hartberg, "AI Writing Disclosures Are a Joke" (Chronicle).

Quick self-check

    Where this comes from

    The Monash model

    This builder adapts Monash University's two-part acknowledgement framework — a widely-used, student-friendly approach to disclosing AI. Full guidance: Acknowledging the use of AI — Monash University.

    Part A — Process documentation

    Keep evidence of how the work came together (drafts, notes, key prompts) so you can show your process if asked. Don't paste it into the assignment — hold it in reserve. This is the answer to exhaustive disclosure: depth on demand, not on the page.

    Part B — Concise summary

    A brief, plain-language account that goes with the work. Monash suggests you:

    • name which AI tools you used and for what
    • link tools you found yourself
    • note roughly how many iterations you ran
    • say how you altered, adopted, or built on the output
    Monash's suggested sentence:  I used AI tool (link) to how used and number of iterations/drafts. I modified the outputs in ways.
    If AI was permitted but you chose not to use it: "No content generated by AI technologies has been used in this assessment."
